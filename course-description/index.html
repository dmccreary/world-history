    AP World History: Modern

    Title: World History: 1200CE to Present

    Target Audience: High school students (grades 10–12) seeking college-equivalent coursework; the course is aligned with introductory college-level modern world history.

    Prerequisites: None. A general comfort with reading primary and secondary historical sources and writing analytical essays is helpful but not required.

    Course Overview

    World History: Modern is an introductory college-level survey of world history from approximately 1200 CE to the present. Rather than memorizing isolated facts, students cultivate the habits of mind of professional historians: analyzing primary and secondary sources, evaluating competing interpretations, contextualizing events within broader patterns of change, and constructing evidence-based arguments about the past.

    The course is organized into nine thematic units that move chronologically from the interconnected civilizations of the medieval world through the emergence of global trade networks, the rise and fall of land-based and maritime empires, the revolutionary upheavals of the 18th and 19th centuries, the catastrophic conflicts of the 20th century, and the accelerating forces of globalization in the contemporary era. Six recurring themes — humans and the environment, cultural developments and interactions, governance, economic systems, social interactions and organization, and technology and innovation — thread through every unit, allowing students to trace long-run patterns across time and geography.

    College Board recognizes AP World History: Modern as equivalent to an introductory college course, and qualifying exam scores (typically 3–5) are accepted for college credit or advanced placement at thousands of universities. The course therefore serves a dual purpose: building genuine historical literacy and critical thinking while creating a measurable credential that strengthens college applications and can reduce time and cost in higher education.

    There are several cross-cutting themes in this course:

    1. Development of critical thinking skills
    2. Cultivation of systems thinking to understand world events holistically
    3. Healthy skepticism about historical claims, including the ability to detect bias and misinformation across sources

    Main Topics Covered

    Unit 1: The Global Tapestry (c. 1200–c. 1450) — 8–10% of exam

    • State formation and political structures in East Asia (Song, Yuan, Ming dynasties)
    • Dar al-Islam: Abbasid Caliphate, regional sultanates, and Islamic governance
    • South and Southeast Asian kingdoms (Delhi Sultanate, Vijayanagara, Majapahit)
    • African states (Great Zimbabwe, Mali Empire, Swahili city-states)
    • Americas civilizations (Aztec/Mexica, Inca, Maya)
    • European feudalism, the Catholic Church, and emerging monarchies
    • Global religions: Islam, Christianity, Buddhism, Hinduism, and their spread

    Unit 2: Networks of Exchange (c. 1200–c. 1450) — 8–10% of exam

    • The Silk Roads: overland trade routes and cultural diffusion
    • The Mongol Empire and Pax Mongolica
    • Indian Ocean trade network: merchants, monsoons, and port cities
    • Trans-Saharan trade routes and the gold-salt economy
    • The Black Death: origins, spread, and demographic consequences
    • Diasporic merchant communities and cross-cultural exchange

    Unit 3: Land-Based Empires (c. 1450–c. 1750) — 12–15% of exam

    • Ottoman Empire: administrative structures, devshirme system, expansion
    • Safavid Empire: Shia Islam as state religion, relations with Ottomans
    • Mughal Empire: religious policy, administration, Akbar's syncretism
    • Manchu (Qing) Empire: conquest, governance of Han Chinese subjects
    • Methods of empire-building: military technology, bureaucracy, tax systems
    • Religion and legitimacy: how rulers used faith to consolidate power

    Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections (c. 1450–c. 1750) — 12–15% of exam

    • European maritime exploration: Portuguese, Spanish, Dutch, English, French
    • The Columbian Exchange: crops, animals, diseases, and demographic collapse
    • Atlantic slave trade: origins, scale, Middle Passage, African participation
    • Colonial economies: encomienda, mita, plantation systems
    • Joint-stock companies (VOC, British East India Company) and mercantilism
    • Cultural and biological mixing: mestizaje, creole societies, syncretism
    • Shifting social hierarchies in colonial contexts

    Unit 5: Revolutions (c. 1750–c. 1900) — 12–15% of exam

    • Enlightenment ideas: natural rights, social contract, reason, secularism
    • Atlantic Revolutions: American, French, Haitian, Latin American independence movements
    • Nationalism and the nation-state
    • The Industrial Revolution: origins in Britain, spread to Europe and North America
    • Technological change: steam power, railroads, textile machinery, steel
    • New economic ideologies: capitalism, socialism, communism (Marx and Engels)
    • Labor movements and changing working conditions

    Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (c. 1750–c. 1900) — 12–15% of exam

    • New imperialism: motives, methods, and ideological justifications
    • Colonization of Africa (the Scramble for Africa, Berlin Conference)
    • British Raj in India and its economic impact
    • Imperialism in East and Southeast Asia: Opium Wars, Meiji Japan's response
    • Indigenous resistance movements: Sepoy Rebellion, Zulu resistance, Boxer Uprising
    • Economic dependency and the world-systems framework
    • Environmental consequences of industrial extraction

    Unit 7: Global Conflict (c. 1900–present) — 8–10% of exam

    • Causes of World War I: nationalism, imperialism, militarism, alliances
    • New warfare technologies: machine guns, poison gas, trench warfare, tanks
    • Russian Revolution and the rise of the Soviet Union
    • The interwar period: Great Depression, rise of fascism, Japanese expansionism
    • World War II: causes, theaters of conflict, the Holocaust, atomic bombs
    • Mass atrocities and genocide as a 20th-century phenomenon
    • The United Nations and post-war international order

    Unit 8: Cold War and Decolonization (c. 1900–present) — 8–10% of exam

    • Cold War origins: Truman Doctrine, Marshall Plan, NATO vs. Warsaw Pact
    • Proxy wars: Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan
    • Nuclear arms race and deterrence doctrine
    • Decolonization in Asia: Indian independence, partition, Vietnamese independence
    • Decolonization in Africa: pan-Africanism, independence movements, challenges
    • Non-Aligned Movement and the "Third World"
    • Collapse of the Soviet Union and the end of the Cold War

    Unit 9: Globalization (c. 1900–present) — 8–10% of exam

    • Technological revolutions: computers, the internet, mobile communication
    • Economic globalization: multinational corporations, free trade agreements, supply chains
    • Global financial institutions: IMF, World Bank, WTO
    • Migration and diaspora communities
    • Climate change and environmental degradation as global challenges
    • Cultural globalization: spread of popular culture, language dominance, resistance
    • Pandemic disease in the 20th–21st centuries
    • Rise of China and the shifting global order

    Topics Not Covered

    • Ancient history (pre-1200 CE): early civilizations, classical antiquity, the ancient Mediterranean — these belong to AP World History: Ancient or comparable courses
    • Deep national history of individual countries beyond their role in global networks (e.g., detailed US domestic political history, detailed Chinese dynastic internal politics beyond their global relevance)
    • AP US History or AP European History content, which treat their respective regions with much greater granularity
    • Pre-1200 CE religious developments, though post-1200 spread and transformation of religions is covered
    • Detailed military tactics, campaigns, or battles (the focus is on causes and consequences, not operational history)
    • Economic theory beyond historical context (no formal macroeconomics or microeconomics)
    • Geography as a standalone discipline (though geographic literacy is assumed)

    Learning Outcomes

    After completing this course, students will be able to:

    Remember

    Retrieving, recognizing, and recalling relevant knowledge from long-term memory.

    • Recall the nine AP World History: Modern units and their approximate date ranges
    • Identify the six core course themes: humans and the environment, cultural developments and interactions, governance, economic systems, social interactions and organization, and technology and innovation
    • Name the major land-based empires of 1450–1750 (Ottoman, Safavid, Mughal, Qing) and their geographic locations
    • Recall key trade routes of the pre-modern world: Silk Roads, Indian Ocean network, trans-Saharan routes
    • List the major causes and phases of World War I and World War II
    • Identify significant historical actors (rulers, thinkers, revolutionaries) and their associated movements or time periods
    • Define key vocabulary: mercantilism, encomienda, devshirme, Columbian Exchange, Pax Mongolica, decolonization, proxy war, globalization
    • Recall the structure of the AP exam (MCQ, SAQ, DBQ, Long Essay) and the historical thinking skills assessed

    Understand

    Constructing meaning from instructional messages, including oral, written, and graphic communication.

    • Explain how the Columbian Exchange reshaped demographics, agriculture, and economies across multiple continents
    • Describe how Enlightenment ideas about natural rights and popular sovereignty fueled Atlantic revolutions
    • Summarize how industrialization in Britain spread to other regions and transformed labor, urbanship, and global trade
    • Explain the relationship between European imperialism and economic dependency in Africa and Asia
    • Describe how the Cold War rivalry between the US and USSR shaped decolonization movements and proxy conflicts
    • Interpret historical maps, charts, and images as evidence of broader historical processes
    • Explain why the Mongol Empire simultaneously facilitated trade and caused catastrophic violence and disease spread
    • Summarize how different empires used religion, bureaucracy, and military force to maintain control over diverse populations

    Apply

    Carrying out or using a procedure in a given situation.

    • Apply the six historical thinking skills (developments and processes, sourcing and situation, claims and evidence, contextualization, making connections, argumentation) when analyzing primary sources
    • Use the HAPP-Y sourcing framework (Historical context, Audience, Purpose, Point of view, and corroboration) to evaluate primary source documents
    • Apply the continuity and change over time (CCOT) reasoning process to trace how a theme evolved across multiple periods
    • Write a thesis statement that makes a historically defensible claim and establishes a line of reasoning
    • Apply comparative analysis to explain similarities and differences between two or more civilizations, empires, or revolutions
    • Categorize historical evidence by type (primary/secondary, quantitative/qualitative) and assess its reliability for a specific argument
    • Construct a short-answer response using specific historical evidence to support a claim

    Analyze

    Breaking material into constituent parts and determining how the parts relate to one another and to an overall structure or purpose.

    • Analyze how geography shaped the development and reach of pre-modern trade networks
    • Analyze the causes of the Industrial Revolution by breaking them into technological, economic, social, and political factors
    • Deconstruct a Document-Based Question (DBQ) prompt by identifying what evidence is needed, what perspective is missing, and how documents corroborate or contradict one another
    • Analyze how nationalist movements in colonized regions drew on and diverged from Enlightenment political theory
    • Examine how social hierarchies (race, gender, class, caste) were constructed, maintained, and contested across different historical contexts
    • Break down the argument structure of a historian's interpretation to identify the thesis, evidence used, and assumptions made
    • Analyze how technological innovations (gunpowder, printing press, steam engine, internet) changed political and economic power structures

    Evaluate

    Making judgments based on criteria and standards through checking and critiquing.

    • Evaluate the reliability and usefulness of a primary source based on its origin, purpose, and historical context
    • Judge the relative significance of multiple causes of a major event (e.g., World War I, the Industrial Revolution)
    • Assess the extent to which a historical claim is supported by available evidence
    • Evaluate competing historical interpretations about contested events (e.g., the role of the slave trade in African development, the legacy of colonialism)
    • Critique the perspective and limitations of official or government-produced historical documents
    • Evaluate the degree to which an empire's administrative policies contributed to its stability or eventual decline
    • Assess how well a student or model essay meets the AP rubric criteria for thesis, contextualization, evidence, and complexity

    Create

    Putting elements together to form a coherent or functional whole; reorganizing elements into a new pattern or structure.

    • Write a fully developed Document-Based Question essay that integrates at least six documents, adds outside evidence, contextualizes the argument, and demonstrates complexity
    • Compose a Long Essay that constructs an original argument, supports it with specific evidence, and explains causation or comparison across time periods
    • Design a comparative timeline that places developments from multiple regions side by side to reveal patterns of convergence and divergence
    • Develop an original historical argument about a topic not directly covered in class, using the analytical skills practiced throughout the course
    • Create an annotated bibliography of primary and secondary sources on a chosen historical controversy
    • Produce a capstone research project or presentation that synthesizes evidence from multiple units to answer a cross-cutting historical question (e.g., "How did disease shape the outcome of colonial encounters across different regions?" or "What role did trade networks play in spreading both ideas and pathogens from 1200 to the present?")
    • Design a thematic essay that traces one of the six course themes across all nine units, demonstrating change over time and continuity

    Assessment and Exam Format

    The AP exam is administered digitally via the Bluebook app and consists of:

    Section Format Time Weight
    Section I-A 55 Multiple Choice Questions 55 min 40%
    Section I-B 3 Short Answer Questions 40 min 20%
    Section II 1 Document-Based Question 60 min 25%
    Section II 1 Long Essay (choice of 3) 40 min 15%

    Why This Course Matters

    AP World History: Modern equips students with skills that transfer far beyond the history classroom. The ability to evaluate sources critically, identify the difference between evidence and assertion, construct a coherent argument, and understand how present conditions were shaped by historical forces are foundational competencies for college coursework in virtually every discipline — from political science and economics to literature and the natural sciences. In an era when information is abundant but poorly contextualized, the analytical habits built in this course are among the most durable investments a high school student can make.
