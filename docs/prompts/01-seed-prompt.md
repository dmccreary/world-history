# Seed Prompt

!!! prompt
    I would like to create a course on world history for high-school students.  What organizations provide lists of concepts that should be covered in a world history course.  My goal is to create a course that has high credibility for my students as they apply for colleges.  I also want to promote critical thinking.

## Response

For a world history course aimed at building college credibility while strengthening critical thinking, you'll want to draw from a layered set of standards: high-credibility frameworks that colleges explicitly recognize, plus pedagogy-focused frameworks that emphasize inquiry. Here are the major organizations to anchor your work.

### Tier 1 — Frameworks colleges recognize directly

College Board — AP World History: Modern. This is the framework with the most direct college admissions credibility, since AP courses are designed as college-equivalent and many universities grant credit for qualifying scores. The course covers world history from c. 1200 CE to the present and is organized around themes including humans and the environment, cultural developments and interactions, governance, economic systems, social interactions, and technology and innovation. The framework has two essential components — historical thinking skills/reasoning processes, and course content organized into commonly taught units. The full Course and Exam Description (CED) is the authoritative concept list. AP Central: https://apcentral.collegeboard.org/courses/ap-world-history College BoardCollege Board

### International Baccalaureate (IB) — History (Diploma Programme). 

The other internationally recognized college-credibility framework. The IB History guide specifies prescribed subjects, world history topics, and HL regional options, and is built around historical investigation and source analysis — a strong match for critical thinking. IB: https://www.ibo.org/programmes/diploma-programme/curriculum/individuals-and-societies/history/

### Tier 2 — National frameworks (U.S.)

#### National Council for the Social Studies (NCSS) — C3 Framework

The College, Career, and Civic Life Framework was developed to help states upgrade their social studies standards and to help districts and curriculum writers strengthen their programs, with explicit goals of building critical thinking, problem solving, and participatory skills for engaged citizenship. It uses an inquiry arc of four dimensions, organized around compelling and supporting questions to guide investigation and application. This is the strongest framework for the critical-thinking goal. NCSS also publishes the National Curriculum Standards for Social Studies (10 themes). https://www.socialstudies.org/standards/c3 Social StudiesSocial Studies

#### National Center for History in the Schools (NCHS), UCLA
National Standards for History. These standards call for a minimum of three years of world history instruction between grades 5 and 12 and advocate courses that are genuinely global in scope. Older (1996) but still influential as a content reference. https://phi.history.ucla.edu/nchs/ Ucla

#### American Historical Association (AHA). 

The largest U.S. professional history organization. The AHA History Tuning Project defines what students should know and be able to do as historians, and the AHA collaborated on the C3 Framework. Useful for grounding your concept list in what professional historians regard as core. https://www.historians.org/

#### World History Association (WHA). 

The dedicated professional society for world historians. Useful for periodization debates and globally balanced framing (avoiding Eurocentrism), which colleges increasingly look for. https://www.thewha.org/

### Tier 3 — Pedagogy-focused, free, standards-aligned
OER Project (World History Project / Big History Project). Founded by historian David Christian and funded by Bill Gates. The curricula are aligned to state standards and develop key historical thinking skills, with student critical thinking and questioning placed front and center. Free, modular, and explicitly designed to feed into AP. Particularly strong for the critical-thinking dimension and for providing a concept graph–like backbone (compelling questions, threshold concepts) that should map well to the learning-graph approach you use in your textbooks. https://www.oerproject.com/World-History OER Project

Stanford History Education Group (SHEG) — Reading Like a Historian. Not a content standards body, but the canonical source for sourcing, contextualization, corroboration, and close reading exercises that operationalize critical thinking. https://sheg.stanford.edu/
State frameworks worth comparing
State frameworks vary in rigor but several are widely respected and well-documented: Virginia Standards of Learning (used by Fairfax County), California History–Social Science Framework (2016), Maryland Modern World History Framework, and the New York State K–12 Social Studies Framework. Minnesota also publishes its own academic standards in social studies that you'll likely need to reference for local credibility.

## A practical recommendation
For maximum college credibility plus critical thinking, I'd suggest using the AP World History: Modern CED as your primary content spine, then overlaying the C3 Framework's inquiry arc as your pedagogical structure, and pulling OER Project / SHEG materials for activities and primary-source work. That combination gives you (a) a defensible concept list colleges recognize, (b) an inquiry-based skeleton that demands critical thinking rather than memorization, and (c) ready-to-adapt open materials.

Given your work on concept dependencies and learning graphs, the AP CED's Key Concepts and the C3 Indicators are both already structured hierarchically and would translate well into a Bloom's-tagged learning graph.
